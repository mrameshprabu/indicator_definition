Peru: Gross Domestic Product: 2007 Prices: BCRP

Source: Central Reserve Bank of Peru

Background

The compilation of national accounts with new base year 2007 present important advances in coverage of compilation of the accounts, in the detail of the transactions, the classification of institutional sectors, and economic activities and products and also made some new conceptual elements recommended by the United Nations in the System of National Accounts 2008.

Quarterly GDP estimates are being publish by the Central Reserve Bank of Peru (BCRP) and National Institute of Statistics and Information Science (INEI). However, BCRP and INEI uses different methodology in calculating their respective GDP. Notable discrepancy can be found in the measurement of exports and imports of goods and services that corresponds to the Central Bank by law and in the components of public consumption.

 

Objective

The Gross Domestic Product intends to measure the total market value of all final goods and services produced within the country in a given period of time.

 

Scope

The data covers the entire economy.  The economic activity sectors considered for the establishment of the quarterly GDP estimates are agriculture and livestock, fishing, mining (broken down into metallic mining and hydrocarbons), manufacturing (broken down into primary and non-primary industries), electricity and water, construction, trade and other services. 

GDP estimated using the expenditure method comprises all the components of aggregate demand: private consumption, public consumption, private investment, public investment, changes in inventory, exports and imports of goods and nonfinancial services.

The Central Industrial Classification of Economic Activities of National Accounts 2007 Base Year is based in Revision 4 of the International Standard Industrial Classification of all Economic Activities (ISIC Rev. 4).

 

Methodology

Estimates for the National Accounts are based on the conceptual framework of the System of National Accounts 2008.

The quarterly data reported by the BCRP include real GDP, which is calculated using the production method and broken down into eight economic activities (agriculture, fishing, mining, manufacturing, electricity and water, construction, trade and services). Product taxes and import duties are included in services. The data is also provided on GDP estimated using the expenditure method and valued at bid prices in current terms and in constant terms (2007 as base year).

Approaches to the Calculation of GDP

Production Approach

It is based on the calculation of production and intermediate consumption of the various industries of the economy. Gross value added of an industry is defined as the difference between Gross Value of Production (basic prices) and the value of intermediate consumption (purchaser prices).

Expenditure Approach

It is based on the calculation of final demand components of GDP. Data on exports and imports of goods and nonfinancial services in nominal terms is collected by the BCRP�s Balance of Payments Division. These data is consistent with those that are used to compile the balance of payments. The International Economy Division provides data on export and import price volumes, values, and indices, as well as the index for the calculation of international inflation.

Data on consumption and public investment in nominal terms is provided by the BCRP�s Public Sector Division. Public consumption includes remuneration and goods and services acquired by general government. Public investment includes investment by general government and public enterprises.

For an estimation of private investment, account is taken of data on construction activities, production, and imports of capital goods, excluding construction materials.

GDP figures on the expenditure side are presented quarterly, adjusting to the values published by the Directorate of National Accounts of the INEI. However, discrepancies arise between the BCRP and the INEI in the measurement of exports and imports of goods and services that corresponds to the Central Bank by law, as well as in the components of public consumption, due to different methodologies to deflate it and take it to real values. The detailed methodology in Real and nominal terms are explained below:

Private Consumption: Private consumption at constant prices is calculated using the supply of goods and services related to consumption and deducting the behavior of inventories. For take it to current prices, the consumer price index is used.

Public Consumption: Information is provided on nominal consumption, disaggregated in salaries and goods and services of the general government. This category includes the central government, government�s local authorities and public institutions such as Regulatory Bodies, EsSalud, the Office of Pension Standardization (ONP), Societies of Public Charity, the National Fund for Public Savings (Fonahpu) and the Consolidated Reserve Fund (FCR).

Gross Domestic Investment: The real levels of the gross fixed investment of the public and private sectors are estimated. The quarterly real private fixed gross investment is calculated by the asset supply method. Based on the evolution of housing construction, imports of capital goods and domestic production of capital goods.

Exports and Imports of Non-financial Goods and Services: The Paasche price indices estimated by the Central Bank are used to calculate exports and real imports of non-financial goods and services. For the current year and for those for which there is no information available from the Accounts Department Nationals of the INEI, the real and nominal variations of the exports and imports of non-financial goods and services were applied, obtained with the methodology described on the levels of the last year published by said institution.

 

Definition(s)

Gross Domestic Product
It�s the total value of the current production of final goods and services within a country over a period of time. Includes therefore the output generated by domestic and foreign residents in the country.

Final Consumption
It uses two concepts of final consumption, final consumption expenditure and effective final consumption: The final consumption expenditure covers the costs incurred by resident institutional units on goods and services for the direct satisfaction of individual or collective needs of community members. The final consumption expenditure may take place on the domestic territory or elsewhere. Effective final consumption includes goods and services purchased by residents to satisfy their individual or collective needs institutional units.

Gross Fixed Capital Formation
It comprises the total value of acquisitions less disposals of fixed assets made by resident producers during the accounting period plus certain additions to the value of non-produced assets derived from productive activity of institutional units. The (tangible or intangible) fixed assets are obtained as a result of production processes, and in turn are used repeatedly or continuously in other production processes for more than a year.

Changes in Inventories
It is the value of the inventories acquired by a company less the value of inventories disposed during the accounting period. Some of these inputs and outputs are attributed to actual purchases or sales, but others reflect internal transactions of the company.

Exports of Goods and Services
All operations (sales, barter, gifts or grants) from which the country's residents supply goods and services to non-residents.

Imports of Goods and Services
All transactions (purchases, barter, gifts or grants) from non-residents which supply goods and services to residents.

 

Release Schedule

National Accounts are published 8 weeks after the end of the reference quarter.
