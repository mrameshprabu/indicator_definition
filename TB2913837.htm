United States: National Income and Product Accounts 2018

Source: Bureau of Economic Analysis

Background

The National Income and Product Accounts (NIPAs) present the value and composition of output produced in the United States for a given period. It provides information to help answer three basic questions. First, what is the output of the economy in terms of its size, its composition, and its use? Second, what are the sources and uses of national income? Third, what are the sources of saving, which provides for investment in future production?

The NIPA estimates are presented in a set of integrated accounts that show U.S. production, income, consumption, investment, and saving. The conceptual framework of the accounts is illustrated by seven summary accounts, and detailed estimates are provided in approximately 300 supporting NIPA tables. It also features some of the most closely watched economic statistics that influence the decisions made by government officials, businesses, and households. Foremost among these estimates is GDP, the most widely recognized measure of the nation�s production. In particular, the quarterly estimates of inflation-adjusted GDP provide the most comprehensive picture of current economic conditions in the United States. Other key NIPA estimates include the monthly estimates of personal income and outlays, which provide current information on consumer income, spending, and saving, and the quarterly estimates of corporate profits, which provide an economic measure of U.S. corporate financial performance.

 

Scope

The value and composition of output is measured using the Gross Domestic Product (GDP), Gross Domestic Income (GDI), Gross National Income (GNI) and personal income. Computing for GDP and GDI should yield similar estimates, as they measure the same thing with the only difference being the former using the final expenditures approach and the latter using the income approach.

Final Expenditure Approach
GDP measures the value of final goods and services produced for sale in the United States for a given period of time. Nonmarket goods and services�such as government provided public education or defense services�are also included in GDP. Household work, child rearing, volunteer work without remuneration and illegal activities are excluded from GDP due to unavailable data that measure the value of these activities.

These components of GDP are valued at their market prices. In cases where market prices do not reflect the value of a good or services, the value is imputed from similar market transactions. Measuring the value of one�s house, for instance, is conducted by imputing its value from renting a similar structure at current prices. If there is no similar good or service to impute the value from, the costs of producing that good (example: building a house requires labor and materials) are estimated.

Also excluded from GDP are the value of sales and intermediate goods. Products that were produced in a given period are included in the GDP whether or not they were bought or sold. Intermediate goods, or inputs used to produce an output�such as the value of wheat and flour in producing bread�are excluded to avoid overstating the value of the final product (which is the bread).

Income Approach
The income approach treats output as the sum of the incomes earned by the government and the owners of capital and labor. Take note that GDI is also GDP but measured using the income approach. A more comprehensive measure of income is the GNI, which is GDI plus income receipts from the rest of the world minus income payments to the rest of the world. Lastly, personal income is the income received by individuals in the form of compensation, proprietor�s income, interest and dividend income, and transfers from government and businesses.

NIPAs exclude capital gains and losses (or gains and losses resulting from the change in price of assets) because this does not represent income from production.

Production boundary
Certain households activities such as housework, do it yourself projects and care of family members are somewhat excluded in SNA and U.S. national accounts due to that the nature of these activities are self-contained and does not have big influence on the economy. Also, the inclusion of these certain household activities affects the usefulness of the accounts for long-standing analytical purposes. The production boundary is further restricted in the US economic accounts by practical considerations about whether the productive activity can be accurately valued or measured.

Asset boundary
|Economic assets are categorized as financial and nonfinancial assets. Financial asset consists of all financial claims, shares or other equity in corporations including gold bullion held by monetary authorities as a reserve asset. These assets are covered in the flow of funds accounts, which are maintained by the Federal Reserve Board.

On the other hand, nonfinancial assets are measured in to two broad categories

1.       Produced assets are assets that have come into existence as a result of a production process.

2.       Non-produced assets are assets that arise from means other than a production process.

Market and nonmarket output
The output that is included in the economic accounts is in the form of �market,� �produced for own use,� or �nonmarket.� Nonmarket output consists of goods and of individual or collective services that are produced by nonprofit institutions and by government and are supplied for free or at prices that are not economically significant.

Geographic coverage
Another important consideration is the geographic boundary that defines what is included in the accounts. In the NIPAs the �U.S. estimates� cover the 50 states and the District of Columbia. This treatment aligns gross domestic product (GDP), the principal measure of U.S. production, with other U.S. statistics, such as population and employment.

Objective

NIPAs enable policymakers, business decision makers, academics and other researchers to understand and monitor the performance of the US economy. Historical data on NIPAs serve as the basis for forecasting models, while key estimates indicate the current state of the US economy and are being used by the White House and Congress in the preparation of budget and tax projections.

NIPAs are also useful in drawing comparisons between the performance of the US economy and other economies. Businesses come up with investment strategies based on this statistic as well.

 

Methodology

The overall structure of the national income and product accounts is in accordance with the principal international guidelines for national accounts, System of National Accounts 2008. Bureau of Economic Analysis (BEA) supports the goal of international harmonization of its national accounts and the NIPA will continue to adopt System of National Accounts (SNA) 2008 to the extent feasible. Nevertheless, because BEA has decided to retain several important NIPA aggregates that do not appear in SNA 2008, some differences will persist. In most cases, the classification system used by the NIPA for sectors, industries and type of product differ from those recommended by SNA 2008. However, improving consistency with SNA 2008 remains an element of BEA's mission of producing accurate, relevant and timely statistics, of responding to the needs of the US user community, customer and of meeting the challenges of a changing economy.

Current Dollar Estimates
The current-dollar, or nominal, estimates are derived from source data that are �value data� which reflect the product of quantity and price. For the estimates that are not derived from value data, separate quantity data and price data�� must be combined. For example, an estimate of expenditures on new autos may be calculated as the number of autos sold times expenditure per auto. An estimate of wages may be calculated as employment times average hourly earnings multiplied to average hours worked, and an estimate of interest received may be calculated as the stock of interest-bearing assets times an effective interest rate.

Adjustments to the Source Data
There are three general types of adjustments to the source data that are incorporated into the NIPA estimates.

1.       Adjustments that are needed so that the data conform to appropriate NIPA concepts and definition.

2.       Adjustments that involve filling gaps in coverage.

3.       Adjustments that involve time of recording and valuation.

Seasonal Adjustment
Quarterly and monthly NIPA estimates are seasonally adjusted at the detailed series level when the series demonstrate statistically significant seasonal patterns. For most of the series that are seasonally adjusted by the source agency, BEA adopts the corresponding seasonal adjustment factors. Seasonal adjustment removes from the time series the average effect of variations that normally occur at about the same time and about the same magnitude each year, for example, weather and holidays. After seasonal adjustment, the cyclical and other short term changes in the economy stand out more clearly.

Three Special Estimation Methods
In cases where primary source data are not available, BEA uses one or more of the following special methods to estimate the values.

1.       Commodity-flow method

2.       Retail control method

3.       Perpetual inventory method

Quantity and Price Indexes
Most of the NIPA estimates are presented in current dollars. Changes in current dollar estimates measure the changes in the market values of goods or services that are produced or sold in the economy. For many purposes, it is necessary to decompose these changes into price and quantity components. Quantities and price are expressed as index numbers with the reference year, at present, the year 2012 equal to 100. Real measures are expressed as index numbers with the reference year 2012 equal to 100. For selected series, they are also expressed in chained (2012) dollars.

Annual Rates
Quarterly and monthly NIPA estimates in current and chained dollars are presented at annual rates, which show the value that would be registered if the rate of activity measured for a quarter or a month were maintained for a full year. Annual rates are used so that periods of different lengths-for example, quarters and years-may be easily compared. Definition(s)

NIPA Entries
The major components of the NIPA's are presented and defined below within the context of the five summary National Income and Product Accounts � domestic product and income account, personal income and outlay account, government receipts and expenditures account, foreign transactions account and saving and investment account. These five summary accounts show the composition of production and the distribution of the incomes earned in production. Each of the components in the summary account can be mapped to one or more of the other summary accounts that make up the full set of NIPA tables. This system of integrated, double-entry accounts provides a comprehensive and unduplicated measure of economy activity within a consistently defined framework. The NIPAs, in combination with Bureau of Economic and Analysis' industry, wealth and regional accounts can be used to trace the principal economic flows among the major sectors of the economy.

Gross Domestic Product (GDP)
The market value of goods and services produced by labor and property in the United States, regardless of nationality.

Gross National Product (GNP)
Gross National Product is the market value of the goods and services produced by labor and property supplied by US residents, regardless of where they are located.

Gross National Income (GNI)
Gross National Income is the cost incurred and the incomes earned in the production of Gross National Product.

National Income
National Income is the sum of all incomes, net of consumption of fixed capital (CFC), earned in production. Includes both factor incomes and nonfactor charges.

Domestic Income
Domestic Income also a net factor-cost measure, is the income that originates in the production of goods and services attributable to labor and property located in the United States.

Personal Income
Personal Income is the income received by persons from all sources, that from participation in production, from both government and business transfer payments and from government interest "Person" consists of individuals, nonprofit institutions that primarily serve individuals, private noninsured welfare funds and private trust funds.

Disposable Personal Income
Disposable Personal Income is personal income less personal tax and nontax payments. It is the income available to persons for spending and saving.

Personal Consumption Expenditures
Personal consumption expenditure is the income received by persons from all sources. It includes income received from participation in production as well as from government and business transfer payments. It is also the sum of compensation of employees (received), supplements to wages and salaries, proprietors' income with inventory valuation adjustment (IVA) and capital consumption adjustment (CCAdj), rental income of persons with CCAdj, personal income receipts on assets, and personal current transfer receipts, less contributions for government social insurance. Related terms: state personal income, local area personal income.

Gross Private Domestic Investment
Gross private fixed investment consists of private fixed investment and change in private inventories.

Consumption of Fixed Capital
Consumption of fixed capital is the charge of the using up of private and government fixed capital located in the United States. It is defined as the decline in the value of the stock of assets due to wear and tear, obsolescence, accident damage and aging.

Personal Outlays
Personal outlay is the sum of personal consumption expenditures (PCE), personal interest payments, and personal current transfer payments.

Personal Saving
Personal Saving is personal income less the sum of personal outlays and personal current taxes. It is the current saving of individuals, nonprofit institutions that primarily serve individuals, life insurance carriers, private noninsured welfare funds, private noninsured pension plans, publicly administered government employee retirement plans and private trust funds.

Final Sales of Domestic Product
Final sales of domestic product is gross domestic product minus change in private inventories. Equivalently, it is sum of personal consumption expenditure, private fixed investment, government consumption expenditures and gross investment and net exports of goods and services.

Gross Domestic Purchases
Gross domestic purchases is the market value of goods and services purchased by US residents, regardless of where those goods and services were produced. It is gross domestic product (GDP) minus net exports of goods and services. Equivalently, it is the sum of personal consumption expenditures (PCE), gross private domestic investment, and government consumption expenditures and gross investment.

Personal Saving as a Percentage of Disposable Personal Income
It is frequently referred to as "the personal saving rate". It is calculated on a monthly, quarterly and annual basis as the ratio of personal saving to disposable personal income.

Gross Saving as a Percentage of Gross National Product
It sometimes referred to as �the national saving rate". It is calculated on a quarterly and annual basis as the ratio of gross saving, the sum of gross private saving and gross national saving to gross national product.

Capital Consumption Adjustment (CCAdj)
It consists of tax-return-based depreciation charges for corporations and nonfarm proprietorships and of historical-cost depreciation (calculated by BEA) for farm proprietorships, rental income of persons, and nonprofit institutions.

Inventory Valuation Adjustment (IVA)
An adjustment made in the national income and product accounts (NIPAs) to corporate profits and to proprietors' income in order to remove inventory "profits," which are more like a capital-gain than profits from current production.

Implicit Price Deflator (IPD)
Is the ratio of the current-dollar value of a series, such as gross domestic product (GDP), to its corresponding chained-dollar value, multiplied by 100.

Advance Estimate
Is the first estimate of gross domestic product (GDP) and its components for a quarter, It is released 25-30 days after the end of the quarter and is based on source data that are incomplete and subject to revision.

Second Estimate
The second estimate of gross domestic product (GDP) and its components for a quarter. It is released 55-60 days after the end of the quarter, and it is based on source data that are more complete than the advance estimate, though they are still subject to revision.

Third Estimate
The third estimate of gross domestic product (GDP) and its components for a quarter. It is released 85-90 days after the end of the quarter, and it is based on source data that are more detailed and comprehensive than the preliminary estimate. The final estimate is still subject to later revisions.

 

Notes and Remarks

In July 2018, the Bureau of Economic and Analysis (BEA) has introduced the 15th comprehensive or benchmark revision of the national income and product accounts (NIPA's). Comprehensive updates are usually conducted at 5-year intervals that correspond with the integration of updated statistics from BEA�s quinquennial benchmark input-output accounts; the last comprehensive update was released in July 2013. The following are the major statistical changes introduced in the NIPAs as part of the comprehensive update.

The major statistical changes that has been introduced in the 2018 comprehensive revision:

1.       Incorporated benchmark input-output accounts for 2012.

2.       Improved seasonal adjustment.

3.       Improved measures of private fixed investment

4.       Harmonized the treatment of government pension plans.

5.       Improved measures of implicit output of savings institutions and credit unions.

6.       Reclassified R&D for software originals from own-account software to R&D.

7.       Recognized capital services in own-account investment in software and R&D.

8.       Reclassified payments made by the Federal Reserve Banks to the US Government as dividend payments.

 

Release Schedule

For GDP and most other NIPA series, quarterly estimates are released on the following schedule:

1.       "Advance" estimates are released near the end of the first month after the end of the quarter, as more detailed and more comprehensive data become available,

2.       "Preliminary" and "Final" estimates are released near the end of the second and third months, respectively.

For gross national product, gross domestic income, national income, corporate profits and net interest, "advance" estimates are not prepared, because of a lag in the availability of source data. The initial estimates for these series are released with the preliminary GDP estimates and the revised estimates are released with the final GDP estimates. Except for the fourth quarter, these estimates are released only with the final GDP estimates.

Monthly estimates of personal income and outlays are released near the end of the month following the reference month; estimates for the preceding 2 to 4 months are subject to revision at that time.

Annual revisions of the NIPA's are usually carried out each summer and cover the months and quarters of the most recent calendar year and of the 2 preceding years. These revisions are timed to incorporate newly available major annual source data. Comprehensive revisions are carried out at about 5 year intervals. They incorporate definitional, statistical and presentational improvements.
