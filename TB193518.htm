South Korea: National Accounts

Source: The Bank of Korea (BOK)

Background

The Bank of Korea has changed the reference year of the South Korean National Income Statistics from 2010 to 2015, and made various changes in source data, concepts and methodologies to better align the Korean National Income Statistics with the recommendations of the 2008 System of National Accounts.

The classification of GDP by kind of economic activity has been changed to correspond to the revisions of the Korea Standard Industry Classification (KSIC) and the International Standard Industrial Classification (ISIC).

 

Objective

National Accounts are statistics that provide a comprehensive and detailed record of the complex economic activities taking place within an economy and of the interaction between the different economic agents, and groups of agents that takes place in markets or elsewhere. Gross Domestic Product (GDP) is the most frequently quoted indicator of national accounts statistics to measure economic performance.

 

Methodology

The following were the recent major changes to the methodology for the 2015 National Accounts:

��   Incorporation of a wide array of new and revised source data such as the Economic Census, 2015 benchmark input-output (I-O) tables and the new series of the balance of payments statement, providing thorough and detailed information on the structure of the Korean economy.

��   Modified non-software companies�� expenditure on self-developed software from an expense to an investment in intellectual property products

��   Incorporated the total trading volume and added value of activities in the digital sharing economy, such as accommodation, ride and talent sharing among individuals, which are taking place via digital intermediary platforms.

��   Expanded the provision of national income distribution statistics with the introduction of mixed income statistics for households

How to seasonal adjustment

Seasonally adjusted statistics are produced through the BOK-X-12-ARIMA program, a Korean version of the U.S. Census Bureau's X-12-ARIMA, and an indirect adjustment method is adopted in which seasonal adjusted data are produced by aggregating seasonally adjusted data of sub-sectors.

Weight

The nominal amount of transactions of the previous year is used being closely related to recent economic conditions.

Compiling Method

In general, National Accounts can be measured from three sides: production, income and expenditure approach

(1) Production Approach

�� The value of domestic gross production, namely gross output, is measured first by industry or commodity, and the value added is calculated by deducting intermediate consumption, which is used in the production process, from gross output

(2) Expenditure Approach

�� The expenditure approach is divided into demand approach and supply approach
The demand approach produces estimates based on expenditure data collected from the final users who directly purchase the final output.
The supply approach is based on supply data linked to final consumers. It discloses how much of the national economy's aggregate supply (total output + import) is used for intermediate demand and final demand according to the Input-Output Table

�� The demand approach method has primarily been used for estimation, and the supply approach  method has been used when there is the problem of a shortage of demand side basic data

(3) Income Approach

�� Income approach estimates national income from the side of income generation
Income approach tallies sample or census data that lists salaries, interest payments, and profits of related enterprises

Revision Policy

Quarterly national income statistics (advance) are firstly revised when preliminary estimates for a particular quarter is developed and secondly revised when annual preliminary and final estimates are developed.

Reference years for national income and national account are revised every five years in consideration of changes in the economic structure, and final estimates are accordingly revised upon the revision of reference years.

Factors causing disparity between preliminary and final estimates are announced upon the release of final estimates, and those between preliminary and advance estimates are announced upon the release of preliminary estimates.

 

Release Schedule

Quarterly advance release: within 28 days after the end of quarter concerned.
Quarterly preliminary release: within 70 days after the end of the quarter concerned
Annual preliminary release: within 3 months after the end of year concerned
Annual final release: within 15 months after the end of year concerned
